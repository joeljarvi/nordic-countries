Iceland

Iceland is a special island country in the North Atlantic Ocean, between North America and Europe. It’s one of the least crowded countries in Europe. The biggest city in Iceland is Reykjavik, where about 36% of the people live. They speak Icelandic there. Iceland is on a special part of the Earth where two big pieces of land meet. This makes it a place with lots of volcanoes and geysers that shoot out hot water. The land has lots of sand, lava fields, mountains, and ice from glaciers. Even though it’s close to the Arctic Circle, the Gulf Stream helps keep it warmer than you might think. Summers are cool, and the weather can be pretty chilly. People started living in Iceland a long time ago, around 874 AD, when a Norwegian named Ingólfr Arnarson was the first person to settle there. Later, other Norwegians and Scandinavians moved there too. Iceland used to have its own government and a very old parliament called the Althing. In the 13th century, Iceland came under Norwegian rule, and later became part of the Kalmar Union with Denmark and Sweden. When that union ended, Denmark controlled Iceland, and Iceland became part of Denmark in 1814. In the early 1900s, Iceland wanted to be its own country, and in 1918, it became the Kingdom of Iceland but still had the same king as Denmark. During World War II, when Denmark was occupied, Iceland decided to become a republic and stop being tied to Denmark. For a long time, Iceland’s people mainly fished and farmed to get by. After World War II, with help from the Marshall Plan, Iceland became richer and developed quickly. Now, it’s one of the richest and most modern countries in the world. It joined the European Economic Area in 1994 and now has a strong economy with finance, biotechnology, and other industries. Iceland has a system that helps everyone with health care and education, and it’s known for having a great quality of life. It’s the smallest country in NATO and doesn’t have a regular army, but it has a coast guard to help keep its waters safe.
